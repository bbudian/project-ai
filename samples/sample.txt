The old lighthouse stood at the edge of the world, where the grey sea met the greyer sky. Every night the keeper climbed the winding stairs, lit the great lamp, and watched the dark water for ships that might need its light. He had done this for forty years, and in all that time he had never once let the lamp go out.

The villagers said the keeper could read the weather in the colour of the waves. When the sea turned the deep green of old glass, a storm was coming. When it shone like hammered silver, the fishing would be good. The children believed he could speak to the gulls, and perhaps he could, for they followed his small boat wherever it went.

One winter a ship came out of the fog with its sails torn and its mast broken. The keeper rowed out through the breaking waves, threw a rope to the frightened sailors, and brought them safely to the rocks below the light. They stayed in the lighthouse until the storm passed, drinking hot tea and telling stories of the far places they had seen: cities of white stone, deserts that sang in the wind, and harbours where the water was warm even in winter.

When spring returned, the sailors mended their ship and made ready to leave. Before they sailed, the captain gave the keeper a small brass compass, worn smooth by many hands. "So you will always know the way home," he said. The keeper kept it on the windowsill beside the lamp, and on clear nights it caught the light and threw tiny stars across the wall.

Years passed. The keeper grew old, and a young woman came from the mainland to learn his work. He taught her to trim the wick, to polish the great lens until it gleamed, and to read the colour of the sea. On his last night he climbed the stairs slowly, lit the lamp one final time, and sat by the window with the brass compass in his hand. In the morning the young keeper found him there, peaceful, with the first light of dawn upon his face.

She kept the lamp burning, as he had taught her, and the ships came and went, and the gulls still followed the small boat across the bay. And on the windowsill the brass compass caught the light, and threw its small bright stars across the wall, year after year, pointing always toward home.
